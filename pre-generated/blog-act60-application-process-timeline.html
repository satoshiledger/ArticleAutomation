How Long Does the Act 60 Application Take? Timeline, Costs, and Tips for 2026 ¿Cuánto Tarda la Solicitud de Ley 60? Tiempos, Costos y Consejos para 2026

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

One of the most common questions prospective Act 60 applicants ask is simple: how long does the whole process take? The honest answer is that it depends—on which chapter you are applying under, how complete your documentation is, and whether the DDEC requests additional information during review. Most applicants receive their decree within 3 to 6 months of submission, though some cases stretch to 10–12 months (InvestPR; Cordasco & Company).

The landscape has shifted meaningfully since 2025. The DDEC audited nearly 1,800 decrees during 2025, issued 305 deficiency notices, annulled 19 decrees, revoked 4 for serious noncompliance, and saw 887 decrees voluntarily surrendered (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025). New screening criteria—including criminal background checks—and stricter pre-approval processes mean applications are receiving more scrutiny than ever. In late 2025, the DDEC also launched its long-awaited Certified Professional program to help accelerate processing by allowing licensed attorneys and CPAs to pre-validate eligibility (News Is My Business, Dec. 2025).

This guide walks through the full application timeline phase by phase, the government fees involved, every document you need, the most common reasons for delays, and actionable tips to streamline approval.

Realistic Timeline: Phase by Phase

The total end-to-end process from initial planning to signed decree typically runs 3 to 12 months, depending on your preparation level and the chapter you are applying under. Here is what each phase looks like in practice:

Phase 1

Pre-Application Preparation

2–8 weeks

This phase includes establishing bona fide residency (if not already a PR resident), forming your Puerto Rico entity (LLC or corporation), registering with SURI, securing a physical office, opening Puerto Rico bank accounts, and gathering all required documentation.

For Individual Investor (Chapter 2) applicants, this also means identifying and beginning the purchase process for your required Puerto Rico residential property (due within 2 years of decree), obtaining a PR driver’s license, and registering to vote.

Phase 2

Application Submission

1–2 weeks

Submit your application through the DDEC Single Business Portal (incentivos.ddec.pr.gov) or SBP (sbp.pr.gov). Pay the non-refundable filing fee. Upload all required documentation. Receive a confirmation number for tracking (LegalClarity; Tax Law Ventures).

As of 2026, applicants must also provide criminal record certifications from their country of previous residence as part of newly enhanced screening requirements (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Phase 3

DDEC Review & Information Requests

2–6 months

This is where the bulk of the wait occurs. The DDEC (or OITE for business decrees) reviews your application against the eligibility criteria for your chosen Act 60 chapter. During this period, the agency may request additional documentation or clarification—these Information Document Requests (IDRs) are extremely common and must be responded to promptly (LegalClarity; InvestPR).

Delays typically stem from incomplete applications, missing documents, slow responses to IDRs, or complex business structures that require deeper analysis. The new Certified Professional program, launched in late 2025, aims to reduce processing times by up to two weeks through pre-eligibility validation by licensed attorneys and CPAs (News Is My Business, Dec. 2025).

Phase 4

Background Verification & Final Review

2–4 weeks

Before the decree is signed, the DDEC now conducts a final background verification. This includes confirming the criminal record certification, verifying that no convictions exist for fraud, serious crimes, or patterns of criminal conduct. In December 2025, a PR Court of Appeals upheld the DDEC’s denial of a decree to an applicant with felony and misdemeanor convictions in California (El Nuevo Día, Dec. 2025).

Phase 5

Decree Issuance & Signing

1–2 weeks

Upon approval, the DDEC issues your Tax Exemption Decree. This is a binding contract specifying your tax rates, exemptions, employment obligations, compliance requirements, and decree term (typically 15 years with 15-year extension option). You review and sign. Your tax benefits are effective from the date specified in the decree.

Key Insight: The application submission date—not the approval date—determines which rules apply to your decree. For those who submitted before December 31, 2025, the pre-HB 505 rules (0% on passive income for Chapter 2) are locked in, regardless of when the decree is actually issued (Cordasco & Company).

The $5,005 Annual Fee Controversy

The annual compliance fee for Chapter 2 Individual Investor decree holders was raised from $300 to $5,005 in 2020, a change that the 20/22 Act Society challenged as unconstitutional. The fee applies retroactively from tax year 2019 forward and is not “grandfathered” for earlier decree holders. While litigation is ongoing, the fee remains in effect and must be paid to maintain good standing (20/22 Act Society; White Coat Investor).

Required Documents Checklist

Having all documents ready before you submit dramatically reduces delays. The DDEC’s most common reason for requesting additional information is simply missing or incomplete documentation. Here is a comprehensive checklist for both major chapters (Tax Law Ventures; LegalClarity; Act 60 Investor):

Chapter 2: Individual Investor

✓ Valid passport (copy)

✓ Social Security card (copy)

✓ PR residential lease or deed

✓ Utility bills at PR address

✓ PR driver’s license

✓ PR voter registration

✓ PR bank account statements

✓ Criminal record certification (from previous jurisdiction)

✓ IRS Form 8898 (filed or ready to file)

✓ Sworn residency declaration

✓ Unconditional acceptance statement

✓ Proof of investment income sources

Chapter 3: Export Services

✓ Entity formation documents (Certificate of Formation)

✓ EIN confirmation letter

✓ SURI registration

✓ PR office lease or ownership documents

✓ Detailed business plan (services, clients, projected revenue)

✓ Description of eligible export activities

✓ Evidence that clients are outside Puerto Rico

✓ Employment plan (if revenue >$3M)

✓ Projected financial statements

✓ Criminal record certification (from previous jurisdiction)

✓ Owner/principal ID documents

✓ PR bank account statements

7 Most Common Causes of Delays (and How to Avoid Them)

Based on DDEC enforcement reports and practitioner experience, these are the issues that most frequently slow down or derail applications:

1. Incomplete or Missing Documentation

The single most common reason for delays. The DDEC will pause review and issue an IDR, and your application goes to the back of the queue until you respond.

Fix: Use the checklist above. Have an attorney or CPA review every document before submission. Missing one page can cost you months.

2. Slow Response to Information Requests

When the DDEC sends an IDR, applicants who take weeks or months to respond push their own timeline back by the same amount—plus re-queue time.

Fix: Respond within 5–7 business days. Have your CPA and attorney on standby during the review period. Treat IDRs as urgent.

3. Criminal Record Issues

Since 2025, all applicants must submit criminal record certifications. The DDEC is now denying decrees to applicants with fraud convictions or serious criminal records—and courts have upheld these denials (El Nuevo Día, Dec. 2025).

Fix: Obtain your criminal background certificate early. If you have any record, disclose it proactively and consult an attorney before applying.

4. Unclear Business Activity Description

For Chapter 3 applicants, vague descriptions of what services are being exported—or failure to demonstrate that services are performed from PR for clients outside PR—triggers additional scrutiny.

Fix: Be specific. Name your clients (or client types), describe the services precisely, explain how they are delivered from your PR office, and confirm no Puerto Rico nexus.

5. No Bona Fide PR Office Established

Applying before securing your physical office or workspace in Puerto Rico raises immediate eligibility questions.

Fix: Sign your office lease before submitting. Include the lease and photos of the workspace in your application package.

6. Applying Under the Wrong Chapter

Some applicants file under Chapter 3 when their income is primarily passive (should be Chapter 2), or vice versa. Misclassification triggers denial or requires a new application.

Fix: Consult with a qualified attorney or CPA to determine the correct chapter before filing. Many applicants need both Chapter 2 and Chapter 3 decrees.

7. Residency Not Yet Established

While you can submit an application while in the process of establishing residency, having no evidence of PR residency at all—no address, no bank account, no driver’s license—weakens your application.

Fix: Complete at least the core residency steps before submitting: PR address, bank account, driver’s license, and voter registration.

2025–2026 DDEC Enforcement: What Changed

The DDEC under Secretary Sebastián Negrón has dramatically increased enforcement. Here is what applicants and decree holders need to know heading into 2026:

1,798 Decrees Audited in 2025

The DDEC audited nearly 1,800 decrees across all chapters during 2025—the most extensive compliance effort in recent years. Results: 305 deficiency notices, 19 annulments, 4 revocations, and 887 voluntary surrenders (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Compliance Audit Committee Formed

A new committee reviews referrals, imposes fines of up to $10,000, and recommends decree revocations. Starting in 2026, the DDEC will issue automatic notifications and escalating fines for late annual report filings, beginning at $1,000 (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Certified Professional Program Launched

In December 2025, the DDEC activated the Certified Professional designation under Act 60 Section 6060.03. Licensed PR attorneys and CPAs can now pre-validate applications and certify compliance, potentially reducing processing times by up to two weeks. The rollout is in four phases (News Is My Business, Dec. 2025; National Law Review).

Criminal Background Screening Required

All Individual Investor applicants must now provide criminal record certifications from their country of previous residence. Final background verification occurs before decree signing. Decrees are not approved for individuals with fraud convictions, serious crimes, or criminal conduct patterns (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025; El Nuevo Día, Dec. 2025).

Most Common Audit Findings

The DDEC reported these as the top compliance failures among existing decree holders: failure to file the Annual Report (IANE), omission of tax payment evidence, lack of proof of PR property purchase, noncompliance with charitable donation requirements, and missing sworn residency declarations (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

HB 505 Reform: What Changed for 2026 Applicants

House Bill 505, signed in 2025, introduced several significant changes effective January 1, 2026 (Cordasco & Company; Christian Kleiner Luxury RE; InvestPR):

4% Tax on Passive Income for New Applicants

New Chapter 2 applicants after January 1, 2026 face a 4% tax rate on capital gains, dividends, and interest (previously 0%). Pre-2026 decree holders are grandfathered at 0%.

Program Extended to 2055

The Act 60 program, previously set to expire in 2035, has been extended through December 31, 2055—adding 20 years of planning certainty.

6-Year Prior Residency Exclusion

New applicants must certify they have not been Puerto Rico residents for the previous 6 years. This ensures the incentive targets new arrivals rather than existing residents.

Chapter 3 Export Services: Unchanged

The 4% corporate tax rate under Chapter 3 remains unchanged for both new and existing applicants. The HB 505 changes primarily affect Chapter 2 Individual Investor decrees.

8 Tips to Speed Up Your Act 60 Approval

1

Hire a PR-licensed attorney or CPA experienced in Act 60. The application process involves legal and tax nuances specific to Puerto Rico. Professional preparation reduces IDRs dramatically. Look for DDEC-certified professionals who can pre-validate your eligibility under the new Certified Professional program.

2

Complete all residency steps before applying. The more evidence of established PR residency you can submit with your application (driver’s license, voter registration, bank statements, utility bills), the fewer questions the DDEC will have.

3

Secure your PR office before filing. For Chapter 3 applicants, having a signed lease and operational office at submission time eliminates a common source of follow-up questions.

4

Obtain criminal background certificates early. These can take weeks depending on the issuing jurisdiction. Having them ready at filing prevents a common bottleneck in the new screening process.

5

Write a clear, specific business plan (Chapter 3). Describe exactly what services you perform, from where, for whom, and why they have no Puerto Rico nexus. Ambiguity creates IDRs.

6

Respond to IDRs within 5–7 business days. Slow responses are the most controllable cause of extended timelines. Keep your attorney and CPA on standby during the review period.

7

Apply for both chapters simultaneously if needed. If you have both passive investment income and an export services business, you may need separate Chapter 2 and Chapter 3 decrees. Filing both together can save overall processing time.

8

Plan for the full compliance lifecycle from day one. Set up your annual report calendar, identify your charitable donation recipients, arrange for CPA-audited financials if applicable, and build your documentation system before the decree is issued—not after.
