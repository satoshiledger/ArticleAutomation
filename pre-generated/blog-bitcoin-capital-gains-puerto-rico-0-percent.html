If you hold a significant amount of Bitcoin and you live on the U.S. mainland, you already know the math: federal capital gains taxes can consume up to 20% of your profits, plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, plus whatever your state charges on top. For a Bitcoin investor sitting on $1 million in unrealized gains, that tax bill could easily exceed $230,000.

Puerto Rico offers a legal, IRS-recognized path to dramatically reduce—or even eliminate—that burden. Under Act 60 of 2019 (Puerto Rico’s Incentives Code), bona fide residents who hold a valid tax exemption decree can pay 0% on capital gains that accrue after they establish residency on the island. Because the IRS classifies Bitcoin as property rather than currency (IRS Notice 2014-21), those gains fall squarely under the same capital-gains framework that Act 60 was designed to benefit.

But this opportunity is not a simple loophole, and it is not without limits. House Bill 505, which passed the Puerto Rico legislature in 2025, changes the rate from 0% to 4% for new applicants beginning in 2026. The IRS has launched an active enforcement campaign targeting Act 60 decree holders, including a high-profile criminal prosecution of an investor who attempted to shelter pre-move gains. In this guide, we break down exactly how the Bitcoin capital gains exemption works, what changed in 2026, what the IRS is looking for, and how to structure your relocation compliantly.

How Bitcoin Is Taxed Under Federal Law

Before understanding how Puerto Rico changes the equation, it is essential to understand the federal baseline. In 2014, the IRS released Notice 2014-21, which established the foundational principle that virtual currency is treated as property for federal income tax purposes (IRS, "Notice 2014-21" sec. 4, Q&A-1). This means that every time you sell, exchange, or otherwise dispose of Bitcoin, you trigger a taxable event—just as you would when selling a stock or a piece of real estate.

The IRS reinforced and expanded this framework in Revenue Ruling 2019-24 and again with Revenue Ruling 2023-14, which addressed the taxability of staking rewards. The agency has continued adding detail through a series of Chief Counsel Advice memoranda and the 2024 digital asset information reporting regulations, making it clear that Bitcoin holders face the same gain-or-loss calculations as holders of any other form of investment property (IRS, "Digital Assets").

Federal Capital Gains Tax Rates on Bitcoin (2026)

Short-term (held < 1 year): Taxed as ordinary income—up to 37% at the highest federal bracket.

Long-term (held ≥ 1 year): Taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%, depending on your taxable income.

Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT): An additional 3.8% surtax applies above certain income thresholds.

State taxes: Add anywhere from 0% (states like Florida and Texas) up to 13.3% (California) on top of federal rates.

For a Bitcoin holder in a high-tax state like California or New York, the combined effective rate on a long-term capital gain can exceed 33%. That is the baseline cost that Puerto Rico’s Act 60 aims to eliminate for qualifying residents.

How Act 60 Eliminates Bitcoin Capital Gains Tax

Puerto Rico’s Act 60 (formally the Puerto Rico Incentives Code, enacted July 1, 2019) consolidated more than 76 separate tax incentive laws into a single unified code. Chapter 2 of the law—the Individual Resident Investor provision, formerly known as Act 22—provides qualifying bona fide residents with a complete exemption from Puerto Rico income tax on three categories of passive income: capital gains, dividends, and interest (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(a)).

Separately, federal law under IRC Section 933 excludes Puerto Rico-sourced income from U.S. federal income tax for individuals who qualify as bona fide residents of Puerto Rico for the entire taxable year (26 U.S.C. § 933). When these two provisions work together, the result is that a qualifying Bitcoin investor who realizes gains after establishing residency can pay 0% to both Puerto Rico and the federal government on those post-move gains.

The Two-Law Framework That Produces 0%

Act 60 Chapter 2 (Puerto Rico law): Exempts PR-sourced capital gains, dividends, and interest from Puerto Rico income tax for decree holders.

IRC Section 933 (federal law): Excludes PR-sourced income from U.S. federal income tax for bona fide PR residents.

Combined effect: Post-move Bitcoin appreciation is exempt from both Puerto Rico and federal income tax, producing an effective 0% rate.

It is critical to understand that this exemption applies only to gains that accrue after you establish bona fide residency in Puerto Rico. Bitcoin appreciation that occurred while you were living on the mainland remains U.S.-sourced income, subject to standard federal capital gains rates. The IRS uses a special 10-year lookback rule under Treasury Regulation § 1.937-2(f) to ensure that pre-move appreciation on investment property is not treated as Puerto Rico-sourced (Holland & Knight).

For Bitcoin specifically, because it is classified as property and gains are sourced to the taxpayer’s residence at the time of sale under IRC § 865, you must be able to demonstrate that you were a genuine bona fide resident of Puerto Rico at the moment of disposition. The IRS has stated it will rigorously enforce this requirement, particularly for crypto traders (Holland & Knight).

Pre-Move vs. Post-Move Gains: The Critical Distinction

The single most important concept for any Bitcoin investor considering Puerto Rico is the distinction between pre-move and post-move appreciation. Getting this wrong is the fastest route to an IRS audit—or worse, a criminal prosecution.

Worked Example: The Pre/Post Split

Suppose you purchased 10 BTC at $20,000 each (total cost basis: $200,000). On the day you establish bona fide residency in Puerto Rico, Bitcoin is trading at $80,000 per coin (total value: $800,000). Two years later, you sell all 10 BTC at $120,000 each (total proceeds: $1,200,000).

Pre-move gain: $800,000 − $200,000 = $600,000 → Subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax (or eligible for the 5% toll tax election).

Post-move gain: $1,200,000 − $800,000 = $400,000 → Exempt under Act 60 + IRC § 933 = 0% tax.

To properly calculate this split, you must establish the fair market value of your Bitcoin holdings as of the date you become a bona fide resident. For Bitcoin and other publicly traded digital assets, this is relatively straightforward—you take a snapshot of the market price on your move date. You should document this with exchange screenshots, timestamped price records, and ideally a contemporaneous memo prepared by your CPA (TokenTax).

The 5% Toll Tax Election

Act 60 Section 2021.01(e) provides an optional election: instead of paying standard U.S. capital gains rates on your pre-move appreciation, you can elect to pay a one-time 5% toll tax to Puerto Rico on the unrealized gain as of your move date. This can be advantageous if your pre-move gains are large and you would otherwise face a combined federal-plus-state rate significantly higher than 5%.

However, the toll tax election requires careful analysis. You must weigh the immediate 5% cost against the possibility that you might never sell the pre-move portion, or that rates could change in the future. A qualified Puerto Rico CPA should model both scenarios for your specific holdings before you make this irrevocable election.

What Changed in 2026: HB 505 and the New 4% Rate

The Puerto Rico legislature passed House Bill 505 in 2025, introducing the most significant change to the Individual Resident Investor program since Act 60 was enacted. The bill was approved by the House of Representatives on April 28, 2025 and by the Senate on June 25, 2025 (San Juan Daily Star).

Key Changes Under HB 505

New 4% tax rate: Individuals who apply for an Act 60 Individual Investor decree on or after January 1, 2026 will pay a 4% fixed tax on capital gains, interest, and dividends—up from the previous 0% (San Juan Daily Star).

Grandfathering clause: Existing decree holders and those who submitted applications before January 1, 2026 retain the 0% rate for the life of their decree—up to 15 years with an optional 15-year extension.

Program extended to 2055: The sunset date moves from 2035 to December 31, 2055, providing decades of planning certainty for new and existing investors.

New 6-year non-residency rule: New applicants must certify they were not residents of Puerto Rico for at least 6 years prior to applying.

Property purchase restriction: Primary residences must be purchased directly by the investor, their spouse, or a trust—purchases through entities like LLCs are no longer permitted (San Juan Daily Star).

For Bitcoin investors applying in 2026 and beyond, the practical impact is that post-move capital gains will be taxed at 4% instead of 0%. While this is a meaningful change, it still represents an enormous reduction compared to mainland rates. A Bitcoin holder in California facing a combined 33%+ rate would still save approximately 29 cents on every dollar of gain by relocating to Puerto Rico under the new structure.

The 4% rate also aligns with the Act 60 Chapter 3 export services corporate rate, creating a more uniform incentive structure across the program. Puerto Rico’s government has framed the change as a step toward long-term program sustainability while maintaining competitive positioning against global tax jurisdictions (News Is My Business).

Qualifying as a Bona Fide Resident: The Three IRS Tests

The entire Bitcoin tax advantage depends on one foundational requirement: you must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico as defined by IRC Section 937 and Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1. The IRS uses three conjunctive tests—meaning you must satisfy all three for the entire taxable year (IRS Publication 570).

Test 1: Physical Presence

You must be physically present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the tax year. Alternative safe harbors exist, including a multi-year calculation of 549 days across three tax years with at least 60 days per year. You should maintain boarding passes, flight records, cell phone location data, and credit card transaction records as documentation.

Test 2: Tax Home

Your principal place of business or employment must be located in Puerto Rico. If you work remotely, your home office in Puerto Rico should be your primary workspace, and your business operations should be centered on the island. You cannot maintain a primary office on the mainland and claim Puerto Rico as your tax home.

Test 3: Closer Connection

You must demonstrate stronger personal, social, and economic ties to Puerto Rico than to any other jurisdiction. This encompasses your primary home, where your family lives, your bank accounts, your voter registration, your driver’s license, your social clubs, your religious affiliations, and your professional memberships. The IRS evaluates the totality of your connections.

In the year you relocate, you must also file IRS Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession) to formally notify the IRS of your change in residency status.

IRS Enforcement Is Real

In July 2023, the IRS announced it had identified approximately 100 individuals claiming Act 60 benefits who did not appear to meet the residency requirements. The agency stated that many of these cases could proceed to criminal investigation (IRS). The most prominent prosecution to date is United States v. Suresh Gajwani, in which an investor pleaded guilty in June 2025 to filing a false document with the IRS in an attempt to shelter approximately $30 million in pre-move capital gains under Act 60. Gajwani was sentenced to one year of probation and agreed to pay approximately $15.3 million in restitution (U.S. Department of Justice).

Beyond Residency: Additional Act 60 Decree Requirements

Passing the three residency tests is necessary but not sufficient. To maintain your Act 60 Individual Investor decree, you must also comply with several ongoing obligations established by the law and enforced by Puerto Rico’s Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC).

Real property purchase: You must purchase residential real property in Puerto Rico within two years of receiving your decree. Under HB 505, this property must be purchased directly by you, your spouse, or a trust—not through an LLC or other entity.

Annual charitable donation: You must donate $10,000 per year to qualifying Puerto Rico-based nonprofit organizations (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(b)(4)).

Annual compliance report: You must file an annual report with the DDEC through the Single Business Portal. Failure to file can result in fines of up to $1,000 per violation and potential decree suspension.

Annual decree fee: The annual filing fee for maintaining your decree is $5,005.

The total annual compliance cost—including the decree fee, charitable donation, and professional advisory fees for a CPA and attorney—typically runs $25,000 to $40,000 per year. For Bitcoin investors with six- or seven-figure annual gains, this cost is generally a fraction of the tax savings.

How Different Bitcoin Activities Are Taxed in Puerto Rico

Not all Bitcoin-related income is treated equally under Act 60. The tax treatment depends on the nature of the activity and whether it qualifies as passive income under Chapter 2 or business income under Chapter 3.

Activity Tax Treatment Rate for Decree Holders
Holding & selling Bitcoin (personal investment) Capital gain under Chapter 2 0% (pre-2026 decrees) / 4% (new decrees)
Staking rewards (personal, small-scale) Passive income under Chapter 2 0% / 4%
Token swaps (post-move tokens only) Capital gain under Chapter 2 0% / 4%
Airdrops received after residency Post-move property; future sales exempt 0% / 4%
Commercial mining (through PR entity) Business income under Chapter 3 4% corporate rate
DeFi yield farming (commercial scale) Business income under Chapter 3 4% corporate rate
Selling pre-move Bitcoin U.S.-sourced capital gain Standard U.S. rates (or 5% toll tax)

The distinction between personal and commercial activity matters significantly. If your Bitcoin staking or DeFi activity rises to the level of a trade or business, the income may need to be routed through a Puerto Rico corporation under Chapter 3’s export services provisions rather than claimed as passive income under Chapter 2. Consult a qualified CPA to determine the correct classification for your specific activities (TokenTax).

Documentation and Record-Keeping: Protecting Your Exemption

In light of the IRS enforcement campaign and the lessons from the Gajwani prosecution, meticulous documentation is not optional—it is essential. The IRS has signaled it will aggressively audit Act 60 participants, and insufficient records can result in your exemption being disallowed and gains being reclassified as U.S.-sourced income (CPA Practice Advisor).

Cost basis snapshot: On the day you establish bona fide residency, document the fair market value of every Bitcoin (and fraction of Bitcoin) you own. Use exchange records, blockchain explorer data, and timestamped price feeds. This establishes the dividing line between pre-move and post-move appreciation.

Lot-level tracking: Tag each Bitcoin lot as “pre-move” or “post-move” in your accounting system. Pre-move lots carry your original mainland cost basis plus the frozen pre-move gain. Post-move lots carry the move-date fair market value as their new basis.

Travel and presence records: Maintain flight manifests, boarding passes, cell phone location data, credit card statements, lease agreements, utility bills, and local membership records. The IRS will use these to verify your 183-day presence.

Annual filing requirements: As a bona fide PR resident, you will file a Puerto Rico Form 482 (reporting worldwide income with Act 60 exempt gains deducted), a U.S. Form 1040 (reporting only U.S.-source and foreign-source income), and your DDEC annual compliance report.

Pro Tip: The IRS cross-references travel records with crypto exchange data. If you sold Bitcoin on a date when your cell phone was pinging a tower in New York, the IRS can argue the gain was U.S.-sourced regardless of your Puerto Rico decree. Sell Bitcoin while physically present in Puerto Rico and keep the documentation to prove it.

The IRS Enforcement Landscape in 2026

The IRS has made Act 60 compliance a stated enforcement priority. In January 2021, the agency launched a dedicated audit campaign targeting Act 60 decree holders through its Large Business & International Division. In 2023, the agency publicly confirmed it had identified roughly 100 individuals who appeared to be claiming benefits improperly (IRS). Since then, the enforcement posture has only intensified.

The Gajwani prosecution in 2025 was the first criminal case to emerge directly from this audit campaign. Gajwani attempted to retroactively convert his Delaware C corporation to an S corporation to take advantage of Act 60 pass-through treatment for pre-move stock gains. The IRS determined the filing was intentionally misleading, and Gajwani ultimately pleaded guilty, facing restitution of approximately $15.3 million (U.S. Department of Justice).

Beyond Gajwani, the Senate Finance Committee has opened its own investigation into Act 60 abuse, with Ranking Member Ron Wyden sending letters to high-profile investors questioning whether pre-move crypto and partnership gains were being improperly sourced to Puerto Rico (Senate Finance Committee).

Top IRS Audit Triggers for Bitcoin Act 60 Holders

Failing the 183-day presence test: The single most common compliance failure.

Selling pre-move Bitcoin and claiming it as post-move: The IRS uses the 10-year lookback rule to verify sourcing.

Using pass-through entities to re-source gains: The IRS has stated S corporations and partnerships cannot invoke § 937 to convert individual-level gains.

Maintaining a U.S. office or tax home: Working primarily from a mainland office undermines the tax home test.

Inadequate closer-connection ties: Keeping your primary bank accounts, driver’s license, voter registration, and family on the mainland.

The Holland & Knight analysis published in November 2025 summarized the current state clearly: Act 60 is a statutory regime built on precise sourcing rules, and the IRS will enforce those rules rigorously, especially for cryptocurrency. The benefits are real, but only for those who fully comply (Holland & Knight).

Step-by-Step: How to Relocate for Bitcoin Tax Savings

If you have determined that the savings justify the cost and lifestyle change, here is a practical roadmap for structuring your relocation compliantly.

1

Engage a Qualified Puerto Rico Tax Team

Before making any moves, hire a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA and a tax attorney experienced with Act 60. They will model your pre-move vs. post-move gains, evaluate the toll tax election, and determine whether Chapter 2, Chapter 3, or both apply to your situation.

2

Document Your Pre-Move Cost Basis

Snapshot the fair market value of every Bitcoin lot you hold as of your intended move date. This creates the legal dividing line between taxable pre-move gains and exempt post-move gains.

3

Apply for Your Act 60 Decree

Submit your application through the DDEC Single Business Portal. Processing typically takes 3 to 6 months. The application fee is $5,005. New applicants in 2026 will be under the 4% regime.

4

Establish Physical Presence and Sever Mainland Ties

Move to Puerto Rico and begin accumulating days toward the 183-day threshold. Change your driver’s license, voter registration, bank accounts, and professional memberships to Puerto Rico addresses. Cancel mainland leases or list mainland property for sale.

5

Purchase Real Property Within Two Years

Acquire residential real property in Puerto Rico in your own name (or your spouse’s name, or through a trust). Under HB 505, purchases through LLCs no longer qualify. This property must be your primary residence.

6

File IRS Form 8898

In the year of your move, file Form 8898 to formally notify the IRS that you have established bona fide residency in Puerto Rico. This is attached to your federal return for the year of relocation.

7

Maintain Ongoing Compliance

Each year: file your PR Form 482 and U.S. Form 1040, submit your DDEC annual report, make your $10,000 charitable donation, pay the annual decree fee, and maintain meticulous presence and sourcing documentation.

Is the Move Worth It? A Cost-Benefit Framework

Puerto Rico is not the right fit for every Bitcoin holder. The decision requires an honest evaluation of both the financial upside and the lifestyle commitment.

Strong Candidates

Bitcoin holders with $500K+ in unrealized post-move gains expected over the decree period

Remote workers or business owners who can genuinely relocate their tax home

Investors willing to commit to 183+ days per year on the island

Individuals paying 25%+ in combined federal and state capital gains taxes

People who are genuinely attracted to the Puerto Rico lifestyle

Weak Candidates

Investors whose gains are primarily pre-move (still subject to U.S. tax or 5% toll tax)

Individuals unable or unwilling to spend 183+ days per year in Puerto Rico

People with strong mainland ties they cannot or will not sever (family, employer, business)

Investors with annual gains under ~$100K (compliance costs may erode savings)

Anyone seeking a “paper” relocation without genuine lifestyle change

As a rough rule of thumb, the breakeven point for most investors—accounting for compliance costs, the real estate requirement, and professional advisory fees—typically falls somewhere around $100,000 to $150,000 in annual post-move capital gains. Above that threshold, the savings accelerate quickly. Below it, you should carefully evaluate whether the lifestyle change and ongoing costs are justified.
