The Bona Fide Residency Test: How to Qualify as a Puerto Rico Resident for Act 60 La Prueba de Residencia Bona Fide: Cómo Calificar Como Residente de Puerto Rico para Ley 60

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Every tax benefit under Act 60—0% capital gains, 4% corporate income tax, tax-exempt dividends—depends on one foundational requirement: you must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico. If the IRS determines that you fail the residency test, none of your income qualifies for the Puerto Rico exclusion under IRC § 933, and you owe U.S. federal tax on everything. It is, as the tax attorneys at Caplin & Drysdale have described it, an all-or-nothing proposition.

Bona fide residency is not the same thing as simply living in Puerto Rico. You do not become a bona fide resident just by spending time on the island. The IRS requires you to satisfy three distinct and conjunctive tests—the presence test, the tax home test, and the closer connection test—every single tax year you claim the benefits (IRC § 937; Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1).

This guide explains each test in detail, walks through the safe harbors and special rules, covers the documentation you need to survive an IRS audit, and addresses the most common mistakes that jeopardize residency claims.

Why Residency Is the Linchpin of Every Act 60 Benefit

The legal mechanism behind Act 60’s tax benefits is straightforward: IRC § 933 provides that a U.S. citizen who is a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for the entire tax year may exclude Puerto Rico-sourced income from U.S. federal gross income. Puerto Rico then taxes that income under its own code—and if you hold an Act 60 decree, Puerto Rico taxes it at the preferential rates (0% or 4%, depending on decree type and timing) rather than at its standard rates of up to 33% for individuals or 37.5% for corporations (IRS Topic 901; Act 60-2019).

The IRS fully appreciates this cliff effect. If residency fails, the entire income exclusion collapses. That is why the IRS launched a dedicated compliance campaign in January 2021 targeting Act 22/60 decree holders, and why their standard Campaign Audit information document request contains over 20 questions focused specifically on whether the taxpayer is a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico (Caplin & Drysdale).

The Stakes Are Real

In the Gajwani case, the taxpayer’s failure to properly establish residency and income sourcing resulted in a guilty plea and $15.3 million in restitution. The IRS is not merely assessing back taxes—it is pursuing criminal prosecution where warranted. Federal prosecutors have even subpoenaed client files from law firms involved in Puerto Rico residency planning (Holland & Knight; CPA Practice Advisor).

Test 1: The Presence Test

The presence test ensures you are physically spending enough time in Puerto Rico. The primary threshold is 183 days during the tax year, but the IRS provides five alternative ways to satisfy this test. You only need to meet one of the five conditions (Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1(c); IRS Publication 570).

Option 1: 183-Day Rule (Most Common)

You were physically present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the tax year. Any part of a day in Puerto Rico counts as a full day of presence. This is the most straightforward path and the one most Act 60 participants rely on.

Option 2: 549-Day Rolling Average

You were present in Puerto Rico for at least 549 days during the three-year period consisting of the current tax year and the two preceding tax years, and you were present in Puerto Rico for at least 60 days during each year of that period. This option helps taxpayers with heavy travel schedules who may occasionally fall short of 183 days in a single year.

Option 3: 90-Day U.S. Ceiling

You were present in the United States (50 states and D.C.) for no more than 90 days during the tax year. Note: international travel days do not count against you under this option.

Option 4: Limited U.S. Earned Income

During the tax year, you had U.S. earned income (wages, salaries, professional fees) of no more than $3,000 in the aggregate and were present for more days in Puerto Rico than in the United States.

Option 5: No Significant U.S. Connection

You had no significant connection to the United States during the tax year. A “significant connection” exists if you maintain a permanent home in the U.S., have current voter registration in a U.S. political subdivision, or have a spouse or minor child whose principal residence is in the U.S. (with limited exceptions for custodial arrangements and students) (Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1(c)(5); Florida Bar).

Day-Counting Rules

The IRS applies specific rules for how days are counted:

Dual-presence days: If you are physically present in both the U.S. and Puerto Rico on the same calendar day (e.g., you fly from New York to San Juan), that day counts as a day of presence in Puerto Rico, not the U.S. (IRS Publication 570).

Medical exceptions: Days you are temporarily in the U.S. to receive (or accompany a parent, spouse, or child receiving) qualifying medical treatment are not counted as days of U.S. presence.

Emergency exceptions: Up to 14 days spent outside Puerto Rico due to a qualifying natural disaster or emergency that prevents your return may be counted as Puerto Rico days.

Pro Tip: Most experienced advisors recommend exceeding 183 days by a comfortable margin—ideally 200 or more days in Puerto Rico—to avoid “close call” years. Travel patterns are often the very first thing reviewed when residency is questioned, and a count of exactly 183 leaves no room for a disputed day (Act60PR.com).

Test 2: The Tax Home Test

The tax home test requires that your principal place of business or employment—your “tax home” under the principles of IRC § 911(d)(3)—be in Puerto Rico, not in the United States or a foreign country (IRC § 937(a)(2); Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1(d)).

Your tax home is generally defined as the entire city or general area where your principal place of business, employment, or post of duty is located, regardless of where you maintain your family home. If you have more than one regular place of business, your tax home is the location of your primary or principal place of business.

For Act 60 participants, this means your primary business operations must genuinely be conducted from Puerto Rico. A mailing address or vacation property on the island is not sufficient. The IRS will look at where business decisions are made, where you regularly work, where employees (if any) are located, and where your office infrastructure actually exists (CPA Practice Advisor; Sabalier Law).

Tax Home Strengtheners

• Maintain a physical office (leased or owned) in Puerto Rico

• Employ at least one Puerto Rico-based employee (required for Chapter 3 decree if revenue exceeds $3M)

• Conduct board meetings, client calls, and day-to-day operations from the island

• Use a Puerto Rico mailing address for all business correspondence

• Keep business records and equipment at your Puerto Rico location

Tax Home Red Flags

• Maintaining a permanent office in a U.S. state from which you conduct business

• Conducting the majority of your work hours from the mainland

• Having your primary business bank accounts at mainland institutions with no Puerto Rico banking

• Listing a mainland address on business licenses, contracts, or professional registrations

Test 3: The Closer Connection Test

The closer connection test is the most subjective of the three and the one that generates the most IRS scrutiny. You must demonstrate that your personal and economic ties are stronger to Puerto Rico than to any U.S. state or foreign country (IRC § 937(a)(2); Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1(e)).

The IRS evaluates this test based on a facts-and-circumstances analysis drawing on factors explicitly listed in the regulations and modeled on the closer connection test used in the substantial presence test context (Treasury Regulation § 301.7701(b)-2(d)). The key factors identified in IRS Publication 570 and the regulations include:

# Factor What the IRS Looks At
1 Permanent home Location of your primary residence. Owning or renting a furnished home in Puerto Rico is critical. Maintaining a mainland home is a significant negative factor.
2 Family Where your spouse, children, and close family members reside. Time spent by family in the U.S. weighs against you.
3 Personal belongings Location of automobiles, furniture, clothing, jewelry, and valuables. Moving belongings to Puerto Rico strengthens the case.
4 Social, political, cultural, religious organizations Memberships in Puerto Rico clubs, churches, civic groups, and charities. Active community involvement in PR is favorable.
5 Business activities Where you conduct business activities other than those defining your tax home. Keeping non-primary business interests in the U.S. can hurt.
6 Banking Location of bank accounts and financial relationships. Primary banking should be in Puerto Rico.
7 Driver’s license Which jurisdiction issued your current driver’s license. Get a Puerto Rico license as soon as eligible.
8 Voter registration Where you are registered to vote. Maintaining U.S. state voter registration is a negative factor. Note: registering to vote in PR requires relinquishing mainland state registration.
9 Resident tax returns Which jurisdiction’s returns you file as a “resident.” Filing Puerto Rico returns as a resident supports the claim.
10 Charitable contributions Where you direct donations. The $10,000 annual donation to a PR nonprofit required by Act 60 Chapter 2 helps here as well.
11 Time spent in the U.S. Even if you pass the 183-day test, spending 170+ days in the U.S. raises questions. The IRS looks at how much time you spend in the U.S. as a de facto indicator (Tom Duffy CPA).

Important: No single factor is dispositive. The IRS evaluates the totality of your circumstances. However, maintaining a permanent home in a U.S. state while claiming Puerto Rico residency is one of the most damaging facts an auditor can find. As one practitioner notes, the IRS is unlikely to heavily weight factors that can be easily manipulated—like joining a local gym—but will focus on deep structural ties like where your family lives and where your primary home is located (Tom Duffy CPA).

Special Rules for the Year You Move

The year you move to Puerto Rico has special rules that make it possible to claim bona fide residency for the full tax year even if you were not present in Puerto Rico for the first part of the year. Under Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1(f)(1), you can be treated as a bona fide resident for the entire year of your move if all three of the following conditions are met:

1. You were not a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico in any of the three tax years immediately preceding the year of your move.

2. For the last 183 days of the year of your move, you did not have a tax home outside Puerto Rico and did not have a closer connection to the U.S. or a foreign country than to Puerto Rico.

3. You are a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for the year of your move plus the two following tax years (i.e., you must remain a bona fide resident for at least three consecutive years total).

Planning Tip: Because condition 2 requires the last 183 days of the year, most advisors recommend moving to Puerto Rico before July 1 to safely satisfy this requirement (PRelocate). If you move on July 1, days 183–365 (July 1 through December 31) is exactly 184 days. Earlier is better to provide a margin of safety.

Filing Form 8898: Notifying the IRS

When you establish (or end) bona fide residence in Puerto Rico, you are required to file IRS Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession) in the year of your move, provided your worldwide gross income exceeds $75,000. This form notifies the IRS of your change in residency status and is filed with your U.S. income tax return (IRS Publication 570; IRS Publication 1321).

Form 8898 asks detailed questions about your closer connection, including questions about your home, family, personal belongings, organizations, banking, driver’s license, voter registration, and where you file as a resident. Part III of the form is specifically titled “Closer Connection to the United States, Foreign Country, or Possession” and mirrors the regulatory factors discussed above.

Failing to file Form 8898 does not technically prevent you from claiming bona fide residency, but it is a compliance requirement that the IRS tracks—and failing to file can draw unwanted attention.

The Audit-Proof Documentation Checklist

The IRS Campaign Audit for Act 60 decree holders typically begins with a standard information document request (IDR) containing approximately 36 questions, the majority of which focus on residency (Caplin & Drysdale). The best defense is contemporaneous documentation that you maintain throughout the year—not records you try to reconstruct after receiving an audit notice.

Presence Test Documentation

• Boarding passes, flight itineraries, and airline loyalty program records

• Passport stamps (for international travel)

• Cell phone location data or records showing which towers your phone connected to

• Credit card and debit card transaction records with location data

• Rideshare (Uber/Lyft) trip history

• Parking receipts, toll records, and E-ZPass/AutoExpreso records

• A contemporaneous day-count log or calendar tracking your physical location each day

Tax Home Documentation

• Puerto Rico office lease or ownership documents

• Puerto Rico employee payroll records

• Business meeting calendars and client call logs

• Corporate resolutions, board meeting minutes conducted from Puerto Rico

• Business licenses and permits from Puerto Rico municipalities

• SURI registration and tax filing confirmations

Closer Connection Documentation

• Puerto Rico residential lease or deed (proof of primary home)

• Puerto Rico driver’s license

• Puerto Rico voter registration (if applicable)

• Puerto Rico bank account statements (primary banking relationship)

• Utility bills at your Puerto Rico address

• Puerto Rico medical and dental records

• Local club memberships, church affiliation, and charitable contribution receipts

• Vehicle registration in Puerto Rico

• School enrollment records (if children attend school in PR)

• Evidence of disposition or rental of any mainland U.S. home

Seven Common Mistakes That Jeopardize Residency

1. Keeping a mainland home “just in case”

Maintaining an available permanent home in a U.S. state is one of the most damaging factors in the closer connection analysis. If you own mainland property, renting it out at fair market value to an unrelated tenant on a long-term lease (and not using it yourself) helps mitigate the risk.

2. Spending too many days in the U.S.

Passing the 183-day test with exactly 183 days while spending 170+ days in the U.S. creates a borderline profile. The IRS considers your time in the U.S. as an important (though unlisted) factor in the closer connection analysis. Aim to keep U.S. days well below 120 per year.

3. Not switching your driver’s license and voter registration

These are easy wins that the IRS specifically asks about on Form 8898. Maintaining an active mainland driver’s license or voter registration is an unnecessary red flag.

4. Failing to maintain a contemporaneous day log

Reconstructing your day count months or years after the fact is difficult and unreliable. Keep a running log or use a travel tracking app to document your location each day.

5. Leaving your family in the U.S.

If your spouse and children remain in a U.S. state while you nominally “live” in Puerto Rico, this creates a very strong argument that your closer connection is to the mainland. The IRS specifically examines how much time your family spends in each location.

6. Running your business primarily from the mainland

Working remotely from a mainland co-working space or home office for a significant portion of the year undermines the tax home test. Your principal place of business must genuinely be in Puerto Rico, supported by records of where you actually work.

7. Returning to the U.S. after a short period

If you move to Puerto Rico and then return to the mainland after only a few years, IRS auditors may retroactively challenge whether your connection to Puerto Rico was ever genuine. The year-of-move special rule requires three consecutive years of bona fide residency. Departing early can unravel benefits claimed in prior years.

Residency Is an Annual Test—Not a One-Time Event

One of the most important things to understand about bona fide residency is that it must be re-established every tax year. You cannot rely on qualifying in prior years to carry forward. Each year, you must independently satisfy all three tests: presence, tax home, and closer connection.

Experienced advisors recommend treating each filing season as a fresh evaluation. Reconcile your travel records, credit card transactions, cell phone data, and flight manifests at the beginning of each year to confirm you met all three tests for the prior year. Identify any close calls early so you can adjust your behavior in the current year (CPA Practice Advisor).

Additionally, remember that meeting the IRS residency tests is separate from—but parallel to—your Act 60 decree obligations. You must also comply with Puerto Rico-side requirements, including the annual DDEC filing, the $10,000 charitable donation (for Chapter 2 decree holders), property ownership, and compliance fees. Failing the decree requirements can result in decree revocation, even if your IRS residency is solid (Act 60-2019, Chapter 12).
