How to Start a Business in Puerto Rico: Step-by-Step Incorporation Guide for 2026 Cómo Iniciar un Negocio en Puerto Rico: Guía de Incorporación Paso a Paso para 2026

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Puerto Rico offers one of the most compelling business environments available to U.S. citizens: a 4% corporate tax rate under Act 60, no federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced income, a bilingual workforce, and direct access to the U.S. legal system—all without giving up your American passport. For entrepreneurs looking to reduce their tax burden while building something real, the island is hard to beat.

But getting set up correctly matters. Puerto Rico has its own Department of State, its own tax authority (Hacienda), its own business registration portal (SURI), and a few structural quirks—such as the fact that Puerto Rico LLCs default to corporate tax treatment rather than pass-through treatment like on the mainland.

This guide walks you through every step of starting a business in Puerto Rico in 2026: choosing the right entity type, filing with the Department of State, obtaining your EIN, registering with SURI, understanding your tax obligations, and positioning your company for Act 60 benefits. Whether you are forming an LLC, a C corporation, or an S corporation, the sequence below will keep you on track.

Step 1: Choose Your Business Structure

The first decision is selecting the right entity type. Puerto Rico recognizes the same core business structures as the U.S. mainland, but the tax treatment and filing requirements differ in important ways. The three most common structures for entrepreneurs relocating under Act 60 are the LLC, C corporation, and S corporation.

Critical Puerto Rico LLC Tax Quirk

Unlike the U.S. mainland, where a single-member LLC is automatically treated as a disregarded entity for tax purposes, Puerto Rico LLCs default to corporate tax treatment under the PR Internal Revenue Code. If you want pass-through or disregarded entity treatment, you must affirmatively file Form 6045 with Hacienda to elect that status. Failing to make this election means your LLC will be taxed as a corporation at PR’s standard rates (up to 37.5%), not at Act 60’s 4% rate, unless you also hold an export services decree (14 LPRA § 3952).

For most Act 60 export services applicants, the LLC is the preferred structure because of its simplicity, lower maintenance requirements, and flexibility. However, if you plan to raise outside capital or need the formal governance structure that investors expect, a C corporation may be more appropriate. Consult your tax advisor before making this decision, as it directly impacts how Act 60 benefits flow to you personally.

Step 2: Reserve Your Business Name

Before you can file formation documents, you need to confirm that your desired business name is available. Puerto Rico requires that entity names be distinguishable from all other registered entities in the Department of State’s Corporations Registry.

For an LLC, the name must include an identifier such as “Limited Liability Company,” “LLC,” “L.L.C.,” “Compañía de Responsabilidad Limitada,” or “CRL” (14 LPRA § 3952). For a corporation, you must include “Corporation,” “Incorporated,” “Corp.,” or “Inc.”

You can search for name availability through the Department of State’s online Corporations Search tool. If your preferred name is available, you can optionally reserve it by filing an Application for Reservation of Corporate Name, which holds the name for a limited period while you prepare your formation documents (PR Department of State).

If you plan to operate under a name different from your legal entity name—for example, doing business as a branded trade name—you will need to file a separate Trade Name Application with the Department of State and pay an additional $150 fee.

Step 3: Appoint a Registered Agent

Every business entity formed in Puerto Rico must designate a registered agent (also called a resident agent) with a physical street address in Puerto Rico. The registered agent is the person or entity authorized to receive official legal notices and service of process on behalf of your company (Act 164-2009, General Corporations Act).

You can serve as your own registered agent if you are a Puerto Rico resident and maintain a physical address on the island (P.O. boxes do not qualify). Alternatively, you can hire a professional registered agent service, which offers the advantages of privacy (their business address appears on the public record instead of yours) and reliability (they maintain regular business hours to accept documents year-round).

Professional registered agent services in Puerto Rico typically cost between $100 and $300 per year. This is a worthwhile investment, especially if you travel frequently or want to keep your home address off the public record.

Step 4: File Your Formation Documents with the Department of State

This is the step that officially creates your business entity. The specific document depends on your chosen structure:

For an LLC: Certificate of Formation

File with the PR Department of State. The filing fee is $250. Required information includes your LLC name, principal office address (physical street address), mailing address, registered agent name and address, business purpose, term of existence (perpetual or a specific date), effective date, and the authorized person’s signature.

Processing time is approximately 1 business day. Expedited options: $100 for guaranteed 24-hour processing, or $500 for 2-hour processing (Active Filings).

For a Corporation: Certificate of Incorporation

File with the PR Department of State. The filing fee is $150. Required information includes the corporate name, corporate purpose, authorized shares and par value, registered agent details, incorporator information, and initial directors.

Same processing timelines as the LLC. Corporations must also draft bylaws and hold an organizational meeting to appoint officers and adopt the bylaws, though these are internal documents not filed with the state.

You can file online through the Puerto Rico Department of State’s electronic portal at estado.pr.gov, by mail, or in person at the Department of State offices in San Juan. Online filing is the preferred and fastest method (PR Department of State, Corporation Registry).

Pro Tip: The registration sequence matters in Puerto Rico. You cannot obtain your Merchant’s Registration from SURI without first having your EIN, and you cannot get your Municipal License without your SURI registration. Follow this order strictly: Department of State → IRS (EIN) → SURI → Municipality (Biz Latin Hub).

Step 5: Obtain a Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)

Once your entity is officially registered with the Department of State, your next step is obtaining a federal EIN from the IRS. This nine-digit number functions as your business’s tax identification number and is required for opening a business bank account, filing tax returns, hiring employees, and registering with SURI.

Puerto Rico businesses are subject to federal employment taxes—including FICA (Social Security and Medicare) and FUTA (Federal Unemployment Tax)—even though Puerto Rico-sourced business income may be exempt from federal income tax under IRC § 933. This means you need an EIN regardless of your Act 60 status.

You can apply for an EIN online through the IRS website (irs.gov), by phone, by fax using Form SS-4, or by mail. The online application is available for entities whose principal business is in the United States or a U.S. territory, and provides an EIN immediately upon completion. Puerto Rico businesses may also use the Spanish-language Form SS-4PR.

EIN Application: Quick Facts

Cost: Free

Online application: Immediate issuance

By fax (Form SS-4): 4 business days

By mail (Form SS-4): 4–5 weeks

Required before: SURI registration, opening a bank account, hiring employees

Step 6: Register Your Business with SURI (Hacienda)

SURI (Sistema Unificado de Rentas Internas) is Puerto Rico’s unified tax administration portal, operated by the Department of Treasury (Hacienda). Every business operating in Puerto Rico must register with SURI before commencing commercial activities—and the registration must be completed at least 30 days before you begin operating. Late registration can trigger a fine of up to $10,000 (CPA Puerto Rico).

Through SURI, you will obtain your Merchant’s Registration Certificate (Certificado de Registro de Comerciante), which authorizes you to collect and remit Puerto Rico’s Sales and Use Tax (IVU). You will also create your business tax accounts for income tax withholding, estimated tax payments, and other obligations.

The registration process is completed entirely online at suri.hacienda.pr.gov. You will need your EIN, your Department of State registration confirmation, and basic business information. The portal is available in both English and Spanish (Piloto 151).

Tax Accounts Created Through SURI

Sales and Use Tax (IVU): 11.5% combined rate (10.5% state + 1% municipal). Monthly filing due by the 20th of the following month.

Income Tax Withholding: Required if you have employees. Withholding tables are published annually by Hacienda.

Estimated Income Tax: Quarterly estimated payments for corporate income tax.

Municipal License Tax (Patente Municipal): Levied on gross receipts by each municipality where you conduct business. Rates vary by municipality.

Once registered, SURI becomes your primary interface for filing returns, making tax payments, and managing your accounts with Hacienda. All IVU returns, income tax returns, and informative declarations are filed electronically through this portal (LegalClarity).

Step 7: Obtain Your Municipal Business License

In addition to registering with the Department of State and Hacienda, you must obtain a Municipal License (Patente Municipal) from the municipality where your business operates. Puerto Rico has 78 municipalities, each with its own licensing requirements and tax rates.

The Municipal License Tax is a tax on gross receipts that varies by municipality, typically ranging from 0.2% to 0.5% of volume of business. Businesses qualifying under Act 60 Chapter 3 (export services) may receive a 50% exemption on this tax, reducing the effective rate further.

Contact the Revenue Office (Oficina de Rentas Internas) of your municipality to apply. You will need your SURI registration confirmation and your Department of State filing as part of the application.

Step 8: Open a Puerto Rico Business Bank Account

A local Puerto Rico business bank account is essential for managing your company’s finances, establishing your presence on the island, and demonstrating to the IRS that your business operations are genuinely based in Puerto Rico. Major banks operating on the island include Banco Popular de Puerto Rico, FirstBanK, and Oriental Bank, along with national banks like Citibank and Chase.

To open a corporate account, you will generally need your Certificate of Formation or Incorporation, your EIN confirmation letter, a copy of your Operating Agreement (for LLCs) or Bylaws and corporate resolution (for corporations), a valid government-issued photo ID for all signatories, and proof of your Puerto Rico business address.

Plan to visit the bank in person for account opening, as Puerto Rico banks typically require an in-person meeting for new corporate accounts. Processing may take 1–2 weeks depending on the institution’s compliance review procedures.

Step 9: Apply for Your Act 60 Tax Decree (If Eligible)

If your business qualifies for Act 60 tax incentives, applying for a decree should happen in parallel with your business formation—not as an afterthought. The decree is the binding contract between your business and the Government of Puerto Rico that grants you the reduced tax rates.

Chapter 3: Export Services (Business Income)

Provides a 4% corporate income tax rate on export services income, 100% tax exemption on dividend distributions from exempt income, 75% property tax exemption, and 50% municipal tax exemption. Your services must be provided to clients located outside Puerto Rico, and most income-generating activities must be performed from your Puerto Rico office (Act 60-2019, Sections 2031.01 et seq.).

Eligible activities include consulting, software development, SaaS, financial advisory, digital marketing, data analytics, AI/ML, blockchain development, architecture, engineering, and many more professional services.

Chapter 2: Individual Resident Investor (Personal Income)

Provides 0% tax on post-move capital gains, dividends, and interest for existing decree holders (grandfathered), or 4% for new applicants in 2026 under HB 505. See our Bitcoin Capital Gains guide for detailed coverage of the individual investor provisions.

Applications are submitted through the DDEC Single Business Portal at incentivos.ddec.pr.gov. Processing typically takes 3 to 6 months. The application fee varies by business size, and the annual compliance filing fee is $505. Businesses with revenue exceeding $3 million must maintain CPA-audited financial statements and employ at least one full-time Puerto Rico resident employee (Act 60-2019, Chapter 3).

Pro Tip: Pay yourself a reasonable salary. One of the top audit triggers for Act 60 export services decree holders is paying yourself an unreasonably low salary while distributing the majority of income as tax-exempt dividends. The OITE (Office of Industrial Tax Exemption) and the IRS both scrutinize this, and an inadequate salary can jeopardize your entire decree.

Step 10: Stay Compliant — Annual and Ongoing Obligations

Forming your business is just the beginning. Puerto Rico has a comprehensive set of annual and ongoing compliance requirements that must be met to keep your entity in good standing and to maintain any Act 60 tax benefits.

Failure to meet these obligations can result in penalties, interest charges, loss of good standing with the Department of State, and—most critically—suspension or revocation of your Act 60 decree. Working with a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA who understands both PR and federal compliance requirements is strongly recommended.
