Puerto Rico’s 4% Corporate Tax Rate: How to Qualify Under Act 60 Export Services Tasa Corporativa del 4% de Puerto Rico: Cómo Calificar Bajo Servicios de Exportación Ley 60

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

Puerto Rico’s standard corporate tax rate can reach 37.5% when you combine the 18.5% normal tax with the graduated surtax. That’s one of the highest corporate rates in any U.S. jurisdiction. But under Act 60 Chapter 3 (Export Services & Commerce), qualifying businesses can drop that rate to just 4%—a reduction of more than 33 percentage points—while also eliminating tax on dividend distributions and slashing property and municipal taxes.

Originally enacted as Act 20 in 2012 and consolidated into Act 60 in 2019, the Export Services program is designed to position Puerto Rico as an international services hub by attracting companies that serve clients outside the island. The concept is simple: if you perform eligible services from a bona fide Puerto Rico office for customers located outside of Puerto Rico, the government rewards you with dramatically reduced taxes locked in by a 15-year decree (Act 60-2019, Chapter 3; McV Attorneys).

This guide explains exactly what it takes to qualify, which activities are eligible, the specific tax benefits, employment requirements, common disqualifiers, and the compliance obligations that keep your decree in good standing.

The Complete Tax Benefits Package

A Chapter 3 Export Services decree provides a comprehensive package of tax incentives, not just the headline 4% rate. The benefits vary slightly depending on whether your company qualifies as a standard business or a Small-to-Medium Business (SMB) with revenue of $3 million or less (Act 60 Solutions; McV Attorneys; Sabalier Law):

Benefit Standard (>$3M revenue) SMB (≤$3M revenue)
Corporate Income Tax4%2% for first 5 years, then 4%
Dividends from Exempt Income100% exempt100% exempt
Property Tax (Real & Personal)75% exemption100% for 5 years, then 75%
Municipal License Tax50% exemption100% for 5 years, then 50%
Decree Term15 years + 15-year extension15 years + 15-year extension
Employment Requirement1+ full-time PR resident employeeNone required

Novel Pioneer Activity: 1% Rate

If your business is engaged in a “Novel Pioneer Activity”—a new type of service not previously offered in Puerto Rico—the income tax rate may be reduced further to just 1%. This designation is determined by DDEC on a case-by-case basis (McV Attorneys).

Decree = Binding Contract

Your Act 60 decree functions as a binding contract between your business and the Government of Puerto Rico. Once issued, the terms cannot be unilaterally modified by the government, even if Act 60 is subsequently amended. This provides certainty that your tax rates are locked in for the full decree term (Act 60-2019; McV Attorneys).

What Activities Qualify as Export Services?

Act 60 Chapter 3 covers a broad range of service-based activities. The DDEC has identified approximately two dozen categories of eligible services. The key principle is that services must be performed from Puerto Rico for clients located outside Puerto Rico, with no Puerto Rico nexus (Act 60-2019, Section 2031.01; Sabalier Law; McV Attorneys):

Technology & Digital

Software development, SaaS, IT/cybersecurity, data analytics, AI/machine learning, blockchain development, app & game development

Professional Services

Consulting, management, accounting, tax preparation, legal support services, engineering, architecture

Finance & Investment

Financial advisory, fund administration, investment management, insurance services, risk management

Marketing & Creative

Advertising, digital marketing, graphic design, PR/communications, publishing, creative industries (design, art, music)

Science & Research

Scientific/laboratory services, R&D, telemedicine (for non-PR patients), educational & training services

Operations & Support

Call centers, shared services centers, centralized management services, business process outsourcing

Important: This list is not exhaustive. The DDEC Secretary has authority to designate additional eligible activities. If your service category is not listed above, consult with a qualified attorney to evaluate whether it may still qualify.

The Four Core Requirements to Qualify

1. Bona Fide Office in Puerto Rico

You must maintain a physical office or establishment in Puerto Rico from which the export services are performed. This can be a dedicated office, a leased space, or even a qualified co-working facility—but it must be a real location where actual work happens, not a virtual mailbox or nominal address (Act 60, Section 2031.01; Dorado Beach Resort).

The IRS and OITE will look at whether your business operations genuinely occur from this location: are employees working there, are client calls being taken there, are business records maintained there?

2. Services Exported Outside Puerto Rico

The services must be provided to clients located outside of Puerto Rico. These can be clients in the U.S. mainland, other U.S. territories, or foreign countries. The critical constraint is that the services cannot have a “connection to Puerto Rico”—meaning they cannot relate to business activities conducted within Puerto Rico, advise on Puerto Rico law, or involve lobbying Puerto Rico government entities (Act 60, Section 2031.01; Starving Artists; CLB Tax).

A U.S.-based software company that relocates its development team to San Juan and continues serving its California-based enterprise clients would typically qualify. A consulting firm that advises Puerto Rico-based businesses on local market strategy would not.

3. Tax Exemption Decree from DDEC

The 4% rate is not automatic. You must apply for and obtain a formal Tax Exemption Decree through the DDEC’s Single Business Portal (incentivos.ddec.pr.gov). This decree is a legally binding contract between your business and the Government of Puerto Rico. Processing typically takes 3 to 6 months (PwC Tax Summaries; InvestPR).

The application requires detailed information about your business activities, projected revenues, employment plans, and Puerto Rico office setup. A filing fee of $5,005 applies (Act 60 Quick Reference).

4. Employment Requirement (If Revenue > $3M)

If your exempt business generates more than $3 million in annual revenue, you must employ at least one full-time Puerto Rico resident employee who directly participates in the business activities covered by the decree. The owner can fill this role if they actually perform the work and receive a bona fide salary (Sabalier Law; McV Attorneys).

If revenue is $3 million or less, there is no employment requirement. However, even without a legal requirement, having at least one PR-based employee strengthens your overall compliance posture and supports both the tax home and bona fide office arguments.

What Does NOT Qualify for the 4% Rate

Understanding the disqualifiers is just as important as knowing the eligible activities. The following types of income and business activities are specifically excluded from Chapter 3 benefits (Act 60, Section 2031.01; Starving Artists; Act 60 Solutions):

Services to Puerto Rico-Based Clients

If your client is physically located in Puerto Rico or the services relate to business activities within Puerto Rico, that income does not qualify. The service must be “exported.”

Passive Investment Income

Capital gains, dividends, and interest income fall under Chapter 2 (Individual Investor), not Chapter 3. These are separate programs with separate decrees.

Services Performed Outside Puerto Rico

If your team physically performs the work from the U.S. mainland or another location outside Puerto Rico, that income is not PR-sourced. Incorporation in Puerto Rico does not determine sourcing—activity does.

PR Government Advisory & Lobbying

Advising on Puerto Rico laws, regulations, or administrative orders, or lobbying Puerto Rico government entities, is specifically excluded.

Retail E-Commerce Selling INTO Puerto Rico

Selling goods for consumption within Puerto Rico does not qualify. Export Trade (Section 2031.02) requires goods sold outside Puerto Rico, and the business must derive at least 80% of gross income from eligible export trade activities.

No Bona Fide PR Office

Running operations from a mainland U.S. office while only incorporating in Puerto Rico will not qualify. You need genuine physical presence and operational activity in Puerto Rico.

The Application Process: Step by Step

1

Establish Your Puerto Rico Entity

Form your LLC or corporation with the PR Department of State, obtain an EIN, and register with SURI. Ensure the entity is properly structured for Act 60 purposes (see our guide on How to Start a Business in Puerto Rico).

2

Set Up Your Bona Fide Puerto Rico Office

Secure a physical workspace—leased office, co-working space (such as Piloto151), or purchased property. The office must be operational before or concurrent with your application.

3

Prepare Your Application Package

Gather documentation including: description of eligible services, projected revenue and employment, evidence of PR office, entity formation documents, and evidence that services are exported outside Puerto Rico.

4

Submit Through DDEC Single Business Portal

File your application at incentivos.ddec.pr.gov. Pay the $5,005 filing fee ($5,000 application fee + $5 processing fee). The DDEC will review your application and may request additional documentation.

5

Receive Your Tax Exemption Decree

Processing typically takes 3 to 6 months. Once approved, your decree is a binding contract specifying your tax rates, exemptions, employment obligations, and compliance requirements for the 15-year term.

6

Begin Operations & Maintain Compliance

Start generating export services income from Puerto Rico. File annual reports with DDEC, maintain segregated accounting for exempt vs. non-exempt income, and keep CPA-audited financial statements if required.

Ongoing Compliance: Keeping Your Decree

Obtaining the decree is only the beginning. Maintaining it requires ongoing compliance with both Puerto Rico-side obligations and federal residency requirements. Failure to comply can result in fines of up to $1,000 per violation or decree revocation (DDEC October 2025 Administrative Release; Tax Law Ventures):

Annual Report to DDEC

File an annual report via the Single Business Portal detailing business activities, revenues, employment, and compliance with decree terms. Annual filing fee of $300–$500 applies.

CPA-Audited Financial Statements

Required if your annual revenue exceeds $3 million. Must be prepared by a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA. Smaller businesses may be subject to review or compilation requirements.

Segregated Accounting

Maintain separate books and records for exempt operations (export services income) and non-exempt operations (any PR-sourced or non-qualifying income). All accounting records must be maintained in Puerto Rico.

OITE Audits

The Office of Industrial Tax Exemption (OITE) conducts compliance reviews at a minimum of once every two years. These audits verify that your operations match the terms of your decree, your income allocation is proper, and your employment requirements are met (Sabalier Law).

Reasonable Salary

Owner-operators must pay themselves a reasonable salary commensurate with the services they provide. Salary income is taxed at regular PR individual rates (up to 33%), not the 4% export services rate. This is one of the top audit triggers for Act 60 decree holders.

The Base Period Income Limitation

If your business was already performing the eligible activity in Puerto Rico before applying for the Act 60 decree, be aware of the Base Period Income rule. This provision limits the benefit by requiring that income earned during the “base period” (the three tax years prior to your decree application) be excluded from the 4% preferential rate. The purpose is to prevent businesses that were already operating in Puerto Rico from simply relabeling existing income to capture the lower rate (McV Attorneys; Act 60 Solutions).

New businesses established specifically to conduct export services from Puerto Rico are not subject to this limitation—which is one reason many relocating entrepreneurs form new entities rather than migrating existing ones.

The Federal Tax Angle: Why Sourcing Matters

The 4% rate is a Puerto Rico-level benefit. The federal tax picture depends on income sourcing and residency. Under IRC § 933, bona fide Puerto Rico residents exclude PR-sourced income from U.S. federal gross income. If your export services income is properly sourced to Puerto Rico (because the services are performed in PR from a bona fide PR office), and you are a bona fide resident, the income is excluded from both U.S. federal tax and taxed at only 4% by Puerto Rico (McV Attorneys; Starving Artists).

The result: a combined effective tax rate of approximately 4% on qualifying export services income, compared to 21%+ for a mainland C-corporation or 37%+ for high-income pass-through owners.

IRS Enforcement Warning: The IRS has made clear it will aggressively scrutinize whether income claimed as Puerto Rico-sourced was genuinely earned through services performed in Puerto Rico. If your team works from the mainland while billing through a Puerto Rico entity, the IRS may treat that income as U.S.-sourced. Incorporation alone does not determine sourcing—where the actual work happens determines sourcing (Holland & Knight; Starving Artists).
