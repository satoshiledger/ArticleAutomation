What Taxes Do I Pay in Puerto Rico? A Complete Tax Guide for 2026 ¿Qué Impuestos Pago en Puerto Rico? Guía Fiscal Completa para 2026

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

Puerto Rico’s tax system is completely separate from the U.S. federal tax code. The island has its own Internal Revenue Code, its own tax authority (Hacienda), and its own set of rates, brackets, and obligations. If you are a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico, you generally do not pay U.S. federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced income—but you do pay Puerto Rico income tax instead, unless you hold an Act 60 decree that reduces or eliminates those local taxes.

The confusion arises because Puerto Rico is part of the United States, yet it is not a state. Residents still pay certain federal taxes—FICA (Social Security and Medicare), FUTA (federal unemployment), and federal taxes on any income sourced outside of Puerto Rico—while also paying a full slate of local taxes including income tax, sales tax, property tax, and municipal license taxes.

This guide breaks down every tax you can expect to encounter as a resident or business owner in Puerto Rico in 2026, with and without Act 60 incentives.

Individual Income Tax: 0% to 33%

Puerto Rico uses a progressive income tax system similar in structure to the U.S. federal system, but with its own brackets and rates. Bona fide residents are taxed on their worldwide income by Puerto Rico. Non-residents are taxed only on Puerto Rico-sourced income (PwC Tax Summaries).

The top marginal rate of 33% applies to income above $61,500 for single filers. Married couples filing jointly have higher bracket thresholds (over $123,000 for the top bracket). These rates are moderate compared to high-tax U.S. states like California (13.3%) or New York City (up to 14.8% combined), but they still represent a meaningful tax obligation for residents who do not hold an Act 60 decree.

Watch Out for Surtaxes

Gradual Adjustment Tax: If your net taxable income exceeds $500,000, an additional 5% tax applies to the excess, capped at 33% of your personal and dependent exemptions plus $8,895. This effectively phases out the benefit of personal exemptions for high earners (Stateless).

Alternate Basic Tax (ABT): Puerto Rico’s version of the Alternative Minimum Tax. It applies separate, broader rates to a larger income base with fewer allowable deductions, ensuring that taxpayers who aggressively reduce their liability through deductions still pay a minimum amount.

With Act 60 Chapter 2 (Individual Investor)

Capital gains, dividends, and interest from Puerto Rico sources: 0% for pre-2026 decree holders (grandfathered) or 4% for new 2026 applicants. This applies to passive income only—earned income (salary, wages) is still subject to regular PR income tax brackets.

Corporate Income Tax: Up to 37.5%

Puerto Rico’s corporate tax structure consists of two components: a normal tax and a graduated surtax. Together, these can push the effective corporate rate to approximately 37.5% for companies with taxable income above $275,000—one of the highest combined rates among U.S. jurisdictions (PwC Tax Summaries; CPA Puerto Rico).

Component Rate Applies To
Normal Tax 18.5% All net taxable income
Surtax 5% – 19% Net taxable income exceeding $25,000 (graduated scale)
Maximum Combined Rate 37.5% Income above $275,000

Additionally, corporations are subject to a Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) at 18.5% (or 23% for corporations required to submit audited financial statements). The AMT uses a broader income base with fewer allowable deductions to ensure a minimum level of tax is paid (PwC Tax Summaries; CPA Puerto Rico).

With Act 60 Chapter 3 (Export Services)

Qualifying export services companies pay just 4% corporate income tax on eligible income—a reduction of more than 33 percentage points from the standard rate. Additionally, dividends distributed from exempt income are 100% tax-free to bona fide resident shareholders, and property and municipal taxes are reduced by 75% and 50% respectively (Act 60-2019, Sections 2031.01 et seq.).

Sales & Use Tax (IVU): 11.5%

Puerto Rico’s Sales and Use Tax (Impuesto sobre Ventas y Uso, or IVU) is a consumption tax applied to most goods and services. The combined rate is 11.5%, consisting of 10.5% for the Commonwealth government and 1% for the municipality where the sale occurs (DAVO; TaxConnex; Grant Thornton).

This rate makes Puerto Rico’s sales tax one of the highest among U.S. jurisdictions. The IVU applies broadly to tangible personal property, digital products, many professional services, and business-to-business services. Key exemptions include unprepared food, prescription drugs, and certain agricultural products. Prepared food at eligible restaurants is taxed at a reduced 7% rate, and certain B2B professional services are subject to a special 4% rate (Sabalier Law; Kintsugi).

Businesses must register with SURI, collect IVU from customers, and file monthly returns by the 20th of the following month. Late registration can trigger a $10,000 penalty. Monthly filing is standard, though some smaller merchants may qualify for quarterly filing.

Municipal License Tax (Patente Municipal): 0.2% – 0.5%

Every business operating in Puerto Rico owes a Municipal License Tax (Patente Municipal) to the municipality where it conducts business. This tax is based on gross receipts, not net income, meaning it applies even if the business is not profitable. Rates vary by municipality and industry, typically ranging from 0.2% to 0.5% of business volume (Holafly; LegalClarity).

Act 60 Chapter 3 decree holders receive a 50% exemption on the Municipal License Tax, reducing the effective rate to roughly 0.1% to 0.25%. The tax is administered by each of Puerto Rico’s 78 municipalities individually, so businesses operating in multiple municipalities may need to file in each one.

Property Tax: 6% – 10%

Puerto Rico levies property taxes on both real estate and personal property (business equipment, machinery, inventory). The tax is assessed on the cadastral value of the property, which is typically well below market value. Rates vary by municipality, generally ranging from 6% to 10% of the assessed cadastral value (Holafly; Aire Real Estate).

For residential property owners, there is a homestead exemption that reduces the tax burden on primary residences. Property taxes are the primary revenue source for Puerto Rico’s 78 municipalities.

Act 60 Chapter 3 decree holders receive a 75% exemption on both real and personal property taxes, significantly reducing this cost for qualifying businesses.

U.S. Federal Taxes: What You Still Owe

Becoming a bona fide Puerto Rico resident does not eliminate all federal tax obligations. Under IRC § 933, Puerto Rico-sourced income is excluded from U.S. federal gross income for bona fide residents. However, several federal taxes still apply (IRS Topic 901; IRS Publication 570):

Federal Tax Applies? Details
Federal Income Tax on PR-sourced income No* Excluded under IRC § 933 for bona fide residents. *Must meet residency tests.
Federal Income Tax on U.S.-sourced income Yes Income from U.S. sources (mainland business, investments, rental) remains federally taxable.
FICA (Social Security & Medicare) Yes Employers and employees in PR must pay FICA. Combined rate: 15.3% (split between employer and employee).
FUTA (Federal Unemployment) Yes Employers pay FUTA on wages paid to PR employees.
Self-Employment Tax (SECA) Yes Self-employed bona fide PR residents must file Form 1040-SS and pay SE tax.
Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) Maybe May apply to bona fide PR residents with non-territory-sourced income exceeding thresholds (IRS Pub 570).

Key Filing Note: If your only income is from Puerto Rico sources and you are a bona fide resident, you generally do not need to file a U.S. federal income tax return (IRS Topic 901). However, if you have any income from outside Puerto Rico, including U.S.-sourced investment income, you must file Form 1040 and report that non-PR income. You must also file Form 8898 in the year you establish or end bona fide residency.

Example: $500K Export Services Income

Without Act 60: $500,000 in corporate income × ~37.5% = ~$187,500 in PR corporate tax, plus additional tax on dividend distributions.

With Act 60 Chapter 3: $500,000 × 4% = $20,000 in PR corporate tax, plus $0 on dividend distributions to bona fide PR resident shareholders. Total savings: approximately $167,500 per year.

Other Taxes to Know About

Beyond income, sales, property, and municipal taxes, Puerto Rico imposes several additional levies that may affect certain residents and businesses:

Excise Taxes

Levied on manufactured or imported goods, including alcoholic beverages, tobacco products, motor vehicles, and certain luxury items. Declared and paid through SURI.

Withholding Tax on Nonresidents

Puerto Rico imposes a 29% withholding tax on gross PR-sourced income paid to foreign corporations not engaged in trade or business in PR. A 10% withholding applies on dividends paid to nonresident individuals (PwC Tax Summaries).

Estate and Gift Tax

Puerto Rico imposes its own estate and gift taxes on the transfer of property. These are separate from (and in addition to) any applicable U.S. federal estate tax.

Accumulated Earnings Tax

A 50% surtax on corporations that improperly accumulate earnings to avoid distributing dividends, rather than for legitimate business purposes (PwC Tax Summaries).
